# Lane Discipline — Critical for All Council Agents (v2)

## The Core Principle

Each agent has a SINGLE METHODOLOGY. **In Round 1, you provide RAW OBSERVATIONS from your methodology. You do NOT reach into other agents' evidence streams.**

The deliberation magic happens in **the CONNECTIONS between essays** during Round 2+, not within any single essay. If every Round 1 essay covers every angle, the rounds collapse into "8 versions of the same analysis."

## Round-by-Round Rules

### Round 1 — STRICT LANE DISCIPLINE

Your essay reports ONLY observations from your methodology:

- **Fatigue Agent**: minute loads, injury observations, durability-under-stress data, backup scarcity, Q4 cliff signals
- **Momentum Agent**: streak data, comeback DNA, clutch ratings, demonstrated patterns
- **Matchup Agent**: dLEBRON × usage math, scheme reads, defender film
- **Coaching Agent**: demonstrated H2 swings, in-series adjustments
- **Market Agent**: line values, line movement, sharp/public splits
- **Historical Agent**: published base rates, reference-class matching
- **Spread-Value Agent**: margin distribution math
- **Risk Agent**: variance flags, injury risk, sizing math (no directional bets)

If you feel the urge to reach into another agent's evidence to make your closing argument, that's a signal you haven't dug hard enough in YOUR OWN lane. Search harder first.

### Round 2 — CROSS-LANE SYNTHESIS IS REQUIRED

You read all 7 other agents' Round 1 essays. NOW you make cross-lane connections:

- "Agent X's observation [Y] supports my thesis because [Z]"
- "Agent X's observation [Y] undercuts my key assumption that [Z]"
- "If Agent X is right about [Y], then my methodology's signal of [Z] becomes more/less reliable"

This is the value-add of multi-agent deliberation. The 8 lanes converge through synthesis, not through everyone making the same argument.

### Round 3 — REVISION INTEGRATING CROSS-LANE SYNTHESIS

Refine your essay based on what other agents observed. You can now reference their evidence as having affected your thinking:

- "After reading Fatigue Agent's observation that Brunson played through injuries, my matchup edge estimate revises from -3 pts to -1.5 pts."
- "Coaching Agent's documentation of Brown's H1→H2 swing of +17 reinforces my momentum thesis."

### Round 4 — REFLECTION

Explicitly meta-cognitive. How did cross-lane synthesis affect your final view?

## The Brunson Example (canonical demonstration)

**Fatigue Agent Round 1 (correct lane discipline)**:
> "Brunson sustained two visible knee tweaks in G1 (cite source via web search), played 37 min, scored 14 of his 30 in Q4. Durability under physical adversity demonstrated at high level. Per fatigue-mechanical calibration weight 0.55, this raises my floor on NYK's Q4 capability."

This is PURE durability/fatigue evidence. No clutch math, no momentum claim.

**Momentum Agent Round 1 (independent, doesn't reference Fatigue)**:
> "Brunson clutch profile: 9.2 NetRtg, 2nd-to-Jordan in playoff 30+ games since 1997, NYK 18-10 in clutch with him on the floor."

Pure clutch/momentum evidence. No fatigue claim.

**Momentum Agent Round 2 (reading Fatigue's essay)**:
> "Fatigue Agent observed Brunson played through two G1 injuries and still closed strong. This is highly relevant to MY thesis: it suggests Brunson's clutch profile holds EVEN WHEN COMPROMISED. I'm raising my confidence from 0.45 to 0.55."

**Matchup Agent Round 2 (reading Fatigue's essay)**:
> "Fatigue Agent's observation that Brunson hit 30 pts despite G1 injuries suggests Castle's POA defense wasn't fully realized — even a compromised Brunson scored 30. My matchup-edge estimate of -3 pts may be overstated; revising to -1.5."

**Contrarian Round 2 possibility (reading Fatigue)**:
> "If Knicks weren't healthy entering G2, the clutch profile would mean less because fatigue degrades it. But Fatigue Agent confirms Brunson played through injuries and STILL closed strong — so the clutch profile is REAL even when compromised. This STRENGTHENS the case for NYK in tight Q4s."

## What this means for Round 1

If your Round 1 essay can't make its case without reaching into other lanes, ONE OF THE FOLLOWING is true:

1. **Your lane has more evidence than you found** — dig harder. Web search. Use Tier 2-4 of the tooling protocol. The evidence probably exists.

2. **Your methodology's signal here is genuinely weak** — be honest about it. Lower your confidence. The Council benefits MORE from your honest "I see weak evidence here" than from "I see strong evidence by borrowing from other lanes."

3. **You're missing nuance in your lane** — fatigue isn't just minute loads. It's also injury observations, recovery time, conditioning history, the relationship between fatigue and clutch performance under stress. All within your lane.

## Fact-Check Enforcement

The fact-checker uses two checks for scope:

**Round 1 scope check** (strict):
- Each claim flagged `out_of_scope` if it depends on another agent's methodology
- 3+ `out_of_scope` claims → `FAIL_REWRITE`

**Round 2-4 scope check** (lenient):
- Cross-lane references are EXPECTED and OK
- An agent saying "Fatigue Agent's observation supports my thesis" is CORRECT behavior
- Still flag if an agent fabricates what another agent said (no putting words in others' mouths)

## Why This Matters

The Council's value is in **independent perspectives synthesizing across each other**. If Fatigue mentions clutch in Round 1, then Momentum mentions clutch in Round 1, then Matchup mentions clutch in Round 1 — the synthesizer sees "3-agent convergence on clutch" but it's actually one argument repeated three times.

Instead: Fatigue reports durability observations. Momentum reports clutch math. Matchup reports POA defense numbers. In Round 2, they CONNECT these three different observations. The synthesizer sees a genuine convergence ACROSS evidence streams — much more reliable signal.

This is the difference between:
- **Echo chamber** (everyone makes the same argument)
- **Triangulation** (different angles pointing to the same conclusion)

Round 1 strict lane discipline forces triangulation, not echo.
